Exploring Johnson Park

As a student myself, I understand very well how hectic academic life can become. One day, I found myself at a dead end, unable to make any progress on my assignments and feeling stressed about the looming deadlines that were getting closer every second. Realizing I needed to get out and away from campus, I went on a search to find a park that was relatively close to my dorm. I came across a park called Johnson Park, that I had never been to before and wanted to check it out. After taking an A bus from the Busch Student Center to the football stadium, I made the remaining journey on foot.

After crossing over a busy road, I entered Johnson Park and the first thing I came across was a sign marking a bike path. The trail was nothing special, just a paved path, and I took a left and headed in that direction. Every so often, I found a bench placed along the path, some with a trash can sitting next to it. Further down the path, I stumbled upon a set of pull-up bars that were built in a section of mulch. Next to them, there was a sign that showed you how to use the bars to do various exercises. Looking down the path, I noticed even more fitness stations with signs explaining their respective exercises. Unfortunately, I couldn’t continue down the bike trail due to some kind of construction that was occurring there, which had that area of the path blocked off.

Still wanting to explore the part of the park on the other side of the construction, I resorted to walking on the side of the road that made its way through the entire park. As I walked, I was frequently passed by runners, alone and in groups, another example of the park’s use as a place of physical fitness. I also came across a stream that ran through the field of grass, with water that seemed to move very slowly, if it even moved at all. Passed the stream, a gazebo was built with brown-painted wood and a shingled roof, which was the perfect place to relax and take in the sights and sounds of the surrounding natural environment. I as a sat in the gazebo, I admired the sparse yet large trees that towered overhead. My gaze eventually brought me back to the ground, where I noticed squirrels scurrying around and robins scattered in a section of grass between the trees.

After looking at the park creatures for some time, I decided to continue along the road to explore another area of the park. At the end of the road there was an open gate that led into a large lot with ample spaces to park, although only a few cars were present there. Continuing alongside the lot, I ran into a restroom building with an oddly shaped roof, making it look like it was wearing some sort of hat. Attached to its wall I found a water fountain. Not the kind of one with questionable water or a button that doesn’t work and even has a spout for filling up water bottles.

As I kept walking past the restrooms, I discovered a rather scenic spot alongside the river that bordered the park. Sitting down on the bench that was located there, I looked out across the river and could see a bridge crossing over it and a couple of buildings peeking over the tree line. Although not a fisher myself, I couldn’t help but think of what a great fishing spot this was.

Realizing there was still more to see, I departed from the riverbank and followed a new trail, this one made of loose gravel. This path was far more curved than the bike path and had me weaving around the many trees that made up this area of the park. I came across what looked like twenty picnic tables, all scattered around beneath the trees. Mixed in with these tables, picnic grills sat in pairs, although using them required one to bring their own fuel source. In close proximity, a large, red-roofed pavilion sat with plenty of additional tables and trash cans underneath.

Right next to the pavilion, there was a small playground with children playing and a couple of parents watching them. This combination of amenities gave this section of the park great potential for a cookout, picnic, or similar gathering of any size. Realizing the gravel trail had taken me back to the same parking lot I had passed earlier, I went out of the gate and back the same way I came. Retracing my steps past the gazebo and the stream, I made it back to the bike trail that had started my exploration. Rather than end my journey there, I decided to see what I could find if I followed the path in the other direction. I passed a couple of barrels that had been repurposed as waste bins, which I liked. The path crossed over a slightly larger stream than the one before by a nice wooden bridge.

On the other side I found a sight that I was definitely not expecting to see. A small group of horses and what I think was a donkey, were tied up to a couple of trees and feasting on hay that coated the grass. Ending my trip to Johnson Park with a nice surprise, I made my way back to the busy road and back to campus.
